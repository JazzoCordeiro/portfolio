How to Make Accessible Websites – WCAG

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) define how to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities. Accessibility covers a wide range of disabilities, including visual, auditory, physical, speech, intellectual, language, learning, and neurological disabilities. Although these guidelines address a wide variety of situations, they are not able to cover the needs of people with all types, degrees, and combinations of disabilities.

Here are some of the main points to consider that will help create better websites and apps. However, if you are interested, you can view the full documentation here: https://www.w3c.br/traducoes/wcag/wcag21-pt-BR/

Tips to make your website more accessible to different types of disabilities.

1. Visual Impairment

People with visual impairments can experience anything from mild difficulties to total blindness. The main WCAG practices for them include:

  • Alternative text (Alt Text) for images: Use textual descriptions for images, graphics, and other visual content, allowing screen readers to interpret the images.
  • Color contrast: Ensure that text has enough contrast against the background, noting that it's luminosity contrast, not color contrast (a minimum of 4.5:1).

TIP: Use textures instead of colors

As mentioned before, basing decisions on color alone is not a good accessibility practice. Whenever possible, visually indicate the difference between elements using primitive shapes or patterns that can be generated from free services or software like Illustrator or Sketch.

  • Semantic structure: Proper use of headings, lists, and headers to facilitate navigation with screen readers.
  • Keyboard-accessible content: Ensure that all content can be accessed without a mouse, using only the keyboard.
  • Avoid blinking or flickering content: Elements that blink more than three times per second can cause discomfort or trigger seizures in people with visual impairments or epilepsy.

2. Hearing Impairment

People who are deaf or hard of hearing may need alternatives for audio content. Practices include:

  • Captions and transcripts: Provide captions for videos and transcripts for audio, ensuring that information is accessible.
  • Visual indicators for audio alerts: For any sound that provides important information, there should also be a visual alternative (such as icons or text).
  • Multimodal content: Ensure that information can be accessed in non-auditory forms, such as text or videos with sign language interpreters (for videos).

3. Motor Impairment

People with motor impairments may have difficulties using a mouse or clicking small links. Some practices include:

  • Keyboard navigation: Ensure that the entire website or app can be accessed using only the keyboard, without needing a mouse.
  • Adequate clickable space (target size): Ensure that buttons, links, and other interactive elements have a minimum size of 44x44 px, making them easier to click.
  • Avoid actions requiring excessive precision: For example, avoid form fields that require small, exact clicks.
  • Customizable interface controls: Allow users to adjust the interface to make it easier to use, such as increasing the size of controls.

4. Cognitive Impairment

People with cognitive impairments may have difficulties understanding or remembering information. Practices include:

  • Simple language: Use clear and simple language, avoiding jargon or complex terms.
  • Clear and clean design: Use simple and consistent layouts without information overload.
  • Clear instructions and guidance: Provide clear instructions and simple explanations for actions like filling out forms or navigating menus.
  • Predictable navigation: The design should be consistent, with easy and clear navigation throughout the site or app.

5. Neurological Impairment / Epilepsy

People with epilepsy or neurological disorders may be sensitive to fast-moving visual stimuli. Practices include:

  • Avoid fast flickering content: Ensure that content that flickers or changes rapidly (more than three times per second) is avoided, as it can induce seizures in people with epilepsy.
  • Control over animations: Offer users the option to pause or disable animations and other moving content.
  • Focus on contrast and non-stressful colors: Use soft contrasts and avoid harsh flashes or effects that might be disturbing to people with neurological conditions.

6. Reading Impairment (Dyslexia)

People with dyslexia have difficulties reading due to letter or word confusion. Practices include:

  • Legible fonts: Use simple and legible fonts, such as Arial, and avoid complex or hard-to-read fonts.
  • Adequate spacing: Provide proper spacing between letters, words, and lines to facilitate reading.
  • Use of contrast and soft colors: Use color combinations that are comfortable for reading, avoiding combinations that cause visual strain.
  • Ability to customize content: Allow users to choose the reading format (e.g., larger fonts, adjusted contrast, or customized backgrounds).

7. Communication Impairment

People with communication impairments may have difficulties interacting with interfaces or expressing themselves. Practices include:

  • Use of text alternatives to voice: Ensure that people can use reading software or voice input, supporting voice commands or other assistive technologies.
  • Ease of filling out forms: Allow forms to be filled out with less effort, such as auto-suggesting fields or pre-filling data.
  • Simplified interaction: Make interactions simple and clear, with fewer steps and elements to select or type.
